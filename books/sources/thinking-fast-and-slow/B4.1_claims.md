| B4.1-C-006 | System 1 avoids uncertainty by inventing plausible causes instead of consulting statistics (a System 2 function). | "Reluctant to turn to System 2 for help, System 1 automatically tries to make it fit and assigns a plausible cause for the aberrant item instead of admitting uncertainty, doubt, and consulting statistics—all functions only System 2 can handle." | I. System 1 and System 2 | fact |
| B4.1-C-007 | WYSIATI (What You See Is All There Is) induces cognitive ease; if System 2 is not jolted out, it endorses false conclusions, causing overconfidence. | "Kahneman calls this What You See Is All There Is, or WYSIATI—a pervasive System 1 tendency that induces cognitive ease. If System 2 is not jolted out of cognitive ease, it will likely endorse the false conclusion. So thinking fast makes one overconfident in quick decisions that ignore critical data for the sake of reinforcing a first impression." | I. System 1 and System 2; Glossary | fact |
| B4.1-C-008 | The halo effect: accepting a favorable first impression and automatically adding more positive qualities; only System 2 can curb it. | "Quick to jump to conclusions, System 1 often falls prey to the halo effect—accepting a favorable impression and automatically adding more for cohesion... It is System 2's job to curb the halo effect, but because System 1 can't determine what data is necessary, it draws conclusions based on the nearest associative data." | I. System 1 and System 2; Glossary | fact |
| B4.1-C-009 | System 1 substitutes simpler heuristic questions for hard questions, and System 2 often accepts the substituted answer without scrutiny. | "To escape complex questions (e.g., 'How happy are you with your life?'), System 1 substitutes simpler, heuristic questions and answers those instead (e.g., 'What is my mood right now?')... It often satisfies System 2 without further scrutiny." | I. System 1 and System 2 | fact |
| B4.1-C-010 | The anchoring effect skews estimates toward a previously stated value, even among experienced experts; System 2 can be trained to adjust away. | "When one hears a number or price (e.g., a listing price on a house), it can influence one's impression of an estimate... Even experienced real estate agents, when asked to estimate a house price after hearing a high anchor, produce inflated estimates. Kahneman suggests training System 2 to adjust away from the anchor." | I. System 1 and System 2; Glossary | fact |
| B4.1-C-011 | High confidence in forecasters is never a reliable measure of accuracy; low confidence may be better because it prompts System 2 engagement. | "A high level of confidence in someone making forecasts is never a reliable measure of accuracy; in fact, low confidence could be better, because it might prompt System 2 to step up to the plate." | I. System 1 and System 2 | fact |
| B4.1-C-012 | Traders' stock-picking success is as random as rolling dice; even when shown the evidence, firms continued rewarding luck as if it were skill. | "But statistics show that traders' success at picking stocks is as random as rolling dice. System 1 and System 2 | fact |
| B4.1-C-013 | Intuitive reasoning is least reliable for decisions requiring predicting the future and assessing risks. | "Intuitive decision making can be effective; however, Kahneman highlights situations in which errors of judgment will occur unless System 2 is called in. Intuitive reasoning is least reliable with decisions that require predicting the future and assessing risks." | Introduction / Understanding | fact |
| B4.1-C-014 | Loss aversion is so powerful it trumps the desire to gain and can lead to costly decisions (prospect theory). | "Decisions involving risk are at the mercy of System 1, which has a high aversion to loss. Loss aversion is so powerful, it even trumps one's desire to gain and can lead to costly decisions, as Kahneman and Tversky's prospect theory shows." | Introduction / Understanding | fact |
| B4.1-C-015 | Standard economics assumes people are rational (Econs), but humans are different — emotions heavily influence financial decisions. | "Standard economics presupposes people are rational beings—which Kahneman calls the Econs—but this does not reflect how humans actually decide things. Emotions play a much more significant role in decision making than most people realize." | II. Humans and Econs | fact |
| B4.1-C-016 | Utility theory is flawed because it ignores reference points; the same wealth state produces different happiness depending on prior gains/losses. | "Kahneman, is flawed because it fails to consider a reference point from which to weigh options before choosing. For example, persons A and B each have five million dollars today. Yesterday, A had nine million dollars and B had one million dollars. According to utility theory, they should be equally happy. However... A would be miserable to have lost four million dollars in one day." | II. Humans and Econs | fact |
| B4.1-C-017 | The endowment effect: people assign greater weight to the pain of losing a possessed item than to the pleasure of gaining it. | "If Professor R bought a rare bottle of wine for thirty-five dollars and Professor T asks to buy it for one hundred dollars, Professor R has to consider the pain of letting it go—this is called the endowment effect... people do not perceive the two points equally, but instead, tend to assign the pain of loss greater weight." | II. Humans and Econs | fact |
| B4.1-C-018 | People overweight the probability of rare events; vivid detail disrupts accurate risk calculation. | "people often overweight the probability of rare events in decision making. Perceiving an unlikely outcome, such as a terrorist attack, with vivid detail can disrupt one's ability to calculate its true risk... The more vivid a possible outcome in one's mind, the more weight the mind applies." | II. Humans and Econs | fact |
| B4.1-C-019 | The disposition effect is selling winning stocks and holding losers; the sunk-cost fallacy is continuing to invest in a losing proposition. | "when one sells a stock to free up some cash, he most likely chooses one that is up over the purchase price. This is the disposition effect... Continuing to invest in a losing proposition is called the sunk-cost fallacy—not a wise choice." | II. Humans and Econs | fact |
| B4.1-C-020 | A better way to alleviate regret is to explicitly anticipate it beforehand; remembering that regret was considered reduces its later effect. | "Kahneman says a better way to alleviate regret is to explicitly anticipate it. If the outcome is bad, remembering that one considered the possibility of regret beforehand will decrease its effect." | II. Humans and Econs | fact |
| B4.1-C-021 | People make more rational choices with broad/joint evaluation framing; single (narrow) evaluations are susceptible to System 1's emotions. | "Kahneman says people make more rational choices when provided a broader, more inclusive frame... Comparative judgments require System 2 and are likely to be more stable than single evaluations, which are susceptible to System 1's emotions." | II. Humans and Econs | fact |
| B4.1-C-022 | Framing can steer decisions based on how options are presented; only engaging System 2 lets one escape emotional biases. | "System 2 is lazy, and without even realizing it, most people can be steered to make decisions, from moral to financial, according to how they are framed... Only by engaging System 2 in decision making can one escape these emotional biases." | II. Humans and Econs | fact |
| B4.1-C-023 | Kahneman recommends combining the outside view with a risk policy; the outside view shifts focus to statistical outcomes of similar situations. | "Kahneman recommends combining the outside view with a risk policy (e.g., never buying extended warranties). The outside view shifts one's focus from the immediate situation to the statistical outcomes of similar situations." | II. Humans and Econs, Examples | fact |
| B4.1-C-024 | The fourfold pattern: risk-averse for probable gains and unlikely losses; risk-seeking for probable losses and unlikely gains. | "the possibility effect—weighting highly unlikely outcomes more than they deserve, and the certainty effect—giving near-certain outcomes less weight... 1. When a gain is probable, the certainty effect makes most people risk averse... 2. When a loss is probable... people willing to take a risk to avoid it..." | II. Humans and Econs, Examples | fact |
| B4.1-C-025 | The remembering self neglects duration in favor of emotional peaks and endings; memory is not ideal for decisions about the future. | "What one actually experiences and what one ultimately remembers are two very different things... stories that typically neglect duration in favor of the emotional peaks that stand out... Memory is not the ideal resource to consult to review the past for decisions about the future." | III. Remembering/Experiencing Self | fact |
| B4.1-C-026 | The peak rule and duration neglect: future decisions are based on a distorted memory weighted by the most intense emotional moment. | "The most intense moment—the emotional peak—was negative, and it has distorted his memory. This is the peak rule. Basing his future musical preferences on that distorted memory is called duration neglect." | III. Examples: peak rule | fact |
| B4.1-C-027 | Affective forecasting is mistaken; the thrill of purchases fades with time (focusing illusion over-weights one factor). | "Sometimes one thinks buying something, like a new car, will bring happiness; Kahneman calls this affective forecasting, pointing out that the thrill fades with time... this is a focusing illusion that over-weights climate and underweights other determinants of well-being." | III. Remembering/Experiencing Self | fact |
| B4.1-C-028 | Organizations make better decisions because they move more slowly and use checklists; individuals can similarly slow down and engage System 2. | "For the very reasons that organizations make better decisions (they move more slowly and employ useful checklists), anyone can prevent errors in their own judgment. By simply slowing down before making important decisions and seeking assistance from System 2, one can broaden the frame for a problem, gather pertinent data, arrive at a decision, reflect upon it, and review it for accuracy." | A Final Word | fact |
| B4.1-C-029 | The Linda conjunction fallacy: even statistically trained people commit it because System 1's plausible story bypasses System 2's probability logic. | "Most picked (b). They were intuitively swayed by the specific details about Linda and bypassed a basic rule of the logic of probability: specifics narrow probability. They committed the conjunction fallacy... 'Feminist bank teller' seemed so plausible to System 1 that System 2's capacity for deciding probability wasn't engaged." | I. Examples: Linda | fact |
| B4.1-C-030 | Priming operates unconsciously; System 2 believes it is autonomous but the mind hides its inner workings from itself. | "These behaviors are unconscious. System 2 believes it is an autonomous agent, but the mind hides its inner workings from itself." | I. Examples: priming | fact |
| B4.1-C-031 | Cognitive ease is a relaxed, gullible state susceptible to illusions; cognitive strain triggers vigilant, analytical System 2 thinking. | "cognitive ease — a relaxed and gullible state in which one can become susceptible to illusions... cognitive strain — a state where one is vigilant, critical and analytical, and leads to rational thinking... | B4.1-C-032 | The best one can do against cognitive illusions is become aware of the most likely situations where they cause errors — constant vigilance is impossible. | "The best way to counteract and avoid these mistakes in System 1 thinking is to actively engage System 2; but since constant vigilance over one's every waking thought is impossible, the best one can do is become aware of the most likely situations where cognitive illusions can cause errors of judgment." | Glossary: cognitive illusions | fact |
